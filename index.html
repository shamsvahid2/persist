03

The science of marketing

Word of mouth isn't one phenomenon — some products spread the way a simple message does, person to person; others need social reinforcement before anyone acts. We use models built for epidemic and contagion research to tell which one your product is dealing with, and design seeding and referral strategy around that answer.

The same logic applies after acquisition: we treat retention as a survival problem — asking not just who will churn, but when — because that question is usually the more useful one to act on.

Healthcare · Topological Data Analysis

Detecting stroke risk before it happens

Working with hospital data on patients with atrial fibrillation, we applied topological data analysis — mapping 5,106 patient records from 15 clinical factors into a shape that groups similar patients together, rather than sorting them into pre-defined buckets.

That shape revealed five distinct patient phenotypes with sharply different stroke risk. It's the same data science we bring to any client's data: don't force it into categories that already exist — let the structure of the data tell you where the real risk groups are.

Marketing Science · Network Contagion

Modeling how word of mouth actually spreads

Not every product spreads the same way. A "simple contagion" moves person to person, like a single recommendation being enough. A "complex contagion" needs reinforcement — someone adopts only after seeing several people they trust do it first.

Using extended persistent homology — a technique from topological data analysis — we built a model that classifies which mechanism is driving adoption on a network, and predicts its parameters with high accuracy, even with the noisy, incomplete data real campaigns produce. Know which one you're dealing with, and seeding, influencer selection, and referral design stop being guesswork.

Retention Science · Survival Analysis

We treat churn like a lifetime, not a label

Most churn models answer one question: will this customer leave, yes or no. We think the more useful question is when — because a customer flagged "likely to churn" six months from now needs a different response than one at risk this week.

We use Kaplan-Meier survival analysis — the same method used to study patient survival in clinical trials — applied to customer lifetime instead. It correctly handles customers who are still active when you're looking (rather than throwing that information away), and lets you compare retention curves across segments: acquisition source, first purchase size, geography, feature adoption.
